Introduction

To start off my monthly resolutions plan, my August resolution was to have a clearer understanding of my finances. More specifically, I outlined a S.M.A.R.T goal:

Specific—I will figure out a method—paper or software—that can help me record my expenses, and view my assets across all my accounts.
Measurable—I will have succeeded if all transactions across all accounts are recorded, all transactions match and all balances on my ledger match with the balances on the online ledgers.
Attainable—I am limiting myself to the month of August to start, so I do not need to record every transaction I have ever made since I was a toddler.
Related—by having everything across all accounts recorded for my own records, I will have clearer understanding of my finances.
Time-bound—I am looking at the month of August, and must have everything done by September 1st. (Su)

I think that I have been successful in this goal. I have looked at several personal finance applications, and determined that GNUCash is right for me. I now have an accurate account of my net worth, as well as the assets and liabilities that go into my net worth. I can audit my expenses, because I have my receipts saved into a folder Receipts, which are linked to the appropriate transactions in GNUCash. As of September 5th (my statement date), all of my transactions are accurate down to the cent.

Requirements

My main requirement going into this project is that I want the solution to be actually used and practical day-to-day. The meme in Fig. 1 resonates with me; it says that simple solutions are often adequate, and mastering the simple solution can often be the right tool for the task (Maddox). In personal finance, I think the ends of the bell curves would be Excel, but finding a personal finance software that is close to the versatility and simplicity of Excel should be the goal.

Defining specific requirements:

More Than a Spreadsheet
The solution needs to have a better workflow, and enough flexibility to do what I could do in a spreadsheet. If the solution doesn’t provide more than a spreadsheet, then there is no reason to use it.
Double-Entry Bookkeeping
In a similar vein, the solution must use double-entry bookkeeping, which improves auditability. Double-entry bookkeeping will be expanded upon later.
Ownership of Data
The solution must let me own my own data. This means having my ledger stored as an easily downloadable file, or directly using the file as the ledger on my devices. Even if my data is only locked behind a free tier, there have been many cases—notably with Quizlet—where a subscription gets added, locking userdata behind a paywall (Freedman).1
Data Portability/Archivability
The solution bust be accessible even if the original application ceases to exist. In practice this means that the data should be stored in a standard format. For this, a good place to start is the Library of Congress Recommended Formats Statement. It recommends line-oriented (TSV or CSV) or open formats such as an SQLite3 database (Recommended Formats Statement: Datasets).
Efficient Workflow
Entering a transaction shouldn’t take longer than it would take to put the same transaction into a spreadsheet.
Fast as a Program
As accounting software has been around now for 40 years, the solution should be light on system resources (CPU, RAM, Storage). Quantitatively, it should take <200MiB of RAM, <10% CPU load.2
Auditability
The solution must be able to keep receipts organized and digitized, so that original documents can be consulted to discover and correct errors.
BONUS: Asset Value Tracking
The solution gains bonus points if there is a way to track the value of securities and currencies.

1Quizlet notoriously locked up data made by users and previously free features behind a paywall. In order to use it as you could before, a $7.99/month subscription is required. Additionally, they removed the option to export your own flashcards as a CSV (Freedman).

There is no reason why an accounting app should take more than 200MiB of RAM and 10% CPU load. Desktop software written in fast compiled languages such as C++, Go, Rust and Haskell can accomplish this easily.

Most Accounting Software Sucks…

For a software application category that is 40 years old, and is just a glorified spreadsheet, it’s honestly impressive how bad most accounting applications are. How is it possible that so many applications mess up UI/UX, when all they need to do is follow the layout and bindings of every spreadsheet software ever made? And how is it that a spreadsheet software can take more system resources than many video games?

To illustrate this, here is a list of the commonly used accounting software that I have tried or looked into, and the downsides that led me to not choose them.

Quicken Simplify and YNAB

Quicken Simplify and YNAB are fully featured, and have all the features I would want:

  • Online banking support
  • Asset price management
  • Cross-platform support
  • Beautiful reports

However, both are also SaaS products that keep all data on the cloud, so I do not own my data, and they are disqualified.

Also, both rely on web interfaces, so while they can run on anything with a web browser, a web browser is also needed to access your own ledger. A web browser can take 1GiB of RAM, with a far slower startup than an optimized native app. It’s unfortunate that Intuit discontinued their stable yearly desktop releases of Quicken, because it looks like that solution would perfectly suit my needs.

Plain-text Accounting

Plain-text accounting or PTA has gained popularity in the last few years with, ledger and hledger. As the name suggests, the idea is that the whole ledger is stored as human-readable plain-text files, meaning your ledger is suited for version control, with minimal line changes.

However, from my—admittedly brief— investigation, it appears that plain-text accounting has some serious downsides:

  1. The claim that plaintext is a better format than popular structured formats is dubious. You lose speed, potential compression of the data, and a lot of tooling that you would normally get for free with something more structured with software that are based on SQLite, XML or JSON.
  2. You get nothing to start, just a couple of command line tools, making it hard to generate reports. What ends up happening is that people create their own tooling, where you would normally get it for free—with an SQLite-based application reports come naturally out of SELECT statements.
  3. Both ledger and hledger do not have a way to attach receipts, reducing auditability.
  4. If a doomsday situation does occur, and we lose the ability to read SQLite, that’s, plaintext does not solve this issue. If we lose access to SQLite, it’s more likely that we also lose access to the ext4 filesystem, and logic behind the SATA connector.

Homebank

Homebank is a pretty, functional accounting application that does double-entry bookkeeping very well. However, for me it doesn't have a way to attach receipts, which is one of the features that I really wanted.

Skrooge

Skrooge from what I gather is similar to HomeBank, but I did not check it out because it tries to increase its search ranking with keywords. For instance, if you search for HomeBank, GNUCash or KMyMoney, Skrooge will also pop up. I think this is poor etiquette, so I did not even bother to download or check it out.

KMyMoney

KMyMoney is really good and feature complete, and might be a bit better than the solution I chose. It has support for an SQLite backend, which increases the portability of data, as well as attaching multiple documents to transactions, double-entry bookkeeping and everything else which is expected. If I wasn’t primarily on GTK, I would absolutely choose KMyMoney

Firefly III

Firefly III is flat-out the most feature-complete software on this list. It comes with amazing reports, a complete API, all types of accounts and many attributes you can put into each and every transaction… it’s just a shame that its UI also by far the worst out of anything on this roundup.

There’s just so much clicking to make anything happen, from reports to adding transactions, to modifying accounts. It makes me think that someone will come along and focus on the UI/UX to make a companion app that's actually really good, but until then it’s not what I will use.

Actual

Actual is a very pretty, very easy to use accounting app. However, the big downside is that it doesn’t offer much more than using a spreadsheet. For one thing, it's a single-entry accounting system, which means that it's much harder to audit expenses. If I lose auditability, I might as well just use a spreadsheet, and that's why I did not choose Actual.

They’re Not Bad Options Though

Despite all that I have said, I understand if you want to use the applications that I chose to skip over. Afterall, Drugs don't work in patients who don't take them (Lindenfield). If using plain-text accounting lets you manage your finances better, leading to a better situation, then so be it. If you don't care about having to open a web browser to view your aggregated financial data, then by all means go ahead. It's better to use a solution than no solution, and all of the applications are capable of doing 90% of what the others can.

GNUCash Does Everything… Unglamorously

The accounting solution I ended up choosing is boring and unglamorous. GNUCash is an old piece of software, with its first stable release in 1998. In the age of 1GiB electron applications, GNUCash’s age is a benefit, not a downside. I get ~100MiB of RAM usage, and less than 3% CPU usage. Additionally, there have been close to 30 years for bugs to be fixed, for people to explore the edges of what’s possible, for developers to expand what’s possible and for an ecosystem of tools to emerge around GNUCash.

Getting started with GNUCash is admittedly a little bit unintuitive, but this is more because it’s also an introduction to double-entry bookkeeping.

Double-Entry Bookkeeping

Double-entry bookkeeping is a bookkeeping strategy that requires every transaction to have an account that money is being taken from, and an account that money is being moved to. In this case, an account can be bank accounts, expense accounts (really more like expense categories), equity accounts and liability accounts (debt). Double-entry bookkeeping helps with auditing, because every cent must be accounted for for the numbers to make sense on both sides or accounts of the transaction.

Speed

GNUCash is written in C and Perl, which makes it suited as a fast desktop application.

Archivability

One really useful part about GNUCash is that I can save my ledger as either a plain-text XML, or as an SQLite database. Weirdly, the XML is a lot smaller than the SQLite database, but I’ll chalk that up to the XML being compressed, and the SQLite currently only saving uncompressed.

The upside of this is that if GNUCash ceases to exist and I want to get all my transactions, it's a matter of writing some SQL. For instance, all transactions can be gotten with:

SELECT * FROM transactions
ORDER BY post_date;

There is a little caveat that some of the information is hidden away in different tables, so if GNUCash really did become abandonware, there would be some effort required to fully reconstruct the ledger, but knowing that it’s very doable with all the information in a single file is comforting to me.

Using GNUCash

It’s one thing to talk about the downsides of different software, it’s another thing to actually learn and use the supposedly best solution. There are some quirks to learn, and it can be a bit confusing at first. I will try to clarify the parts that confused me, but if there are parts that are still confusing, feel free to reach out.

Setting Up Your File

To start with GNUCash, create a new file by going to File→New File. Most of this is self-explanatory. When choosing which accounts to create, don’t worry too much about getting all the accounts right. You can refine them to what you care about after-the-fact. Finally, when saving, choose either XML or SQLite. I recommend SQLite, because it’s a lot more portable.1

1SQLite is really nice, because you can use standard SQLite tools such as DB4S or DBeaver to view your data. For instance, transactions can be accessed from the transactions table. I have noticed that the size of SQLite databases are significantly larger than with XML—for my ledger including just August data—XML took 30kiB, and SQLite took 500kiB. This seems like a lot until you extrapolate it to a time period. Assuming you use GNUCash for 40 years, you would use: 500KiB months-1 · 12 months · 40 years = 240MiB which I think is an acceptible amount of space for a lifetime of finances. Of course, with the XML the same calculation gives 14.4MiB, which is even better. The discrepency in size is because the XML is compressed, and the SQLite is not (GNUCash Development Team). In the far future, GNUCash may add compression for SQLite databases, but it does’t appear to be on any developer’s radar right now.

To use SQLite on Linux, you may need to install another package. For instance, on Debian you need to install the libdbd-sqlite3.

My Accounts

I set up accounts for all my assets, equity, expenses, income and liabilities.

Assets
For assets, I have pretty standard accounts, as well as my Venmo account. I don’t think Venmo fits very well into standard accounts, so I just put it as a Bank account, because usually I will have more money in Venmo than I take out. Additionally, I have assets split into liquid and non-liquid, which to me just means accounts that get used vs accounts that sit around to accrue money.
Equity
I honestly don’t know what equity really means in this context. To me, it’s just a slush fund for opening balances and nothing else.
Expenses
I found that the default expense categories didn’t align with what I actually care about. Currently, I have major expense categories Everything Else, Me, Social, Town House and Transit. It takes a bit of a fine-tuning, but if you change.
Income
I have pretty standard accounts for capital gains, work, gifts and other income—such as selling things on EBay.
Liabilities
For liabilities, I have my car loan, credit cards and my house’s Splitwise. I put Splitwise in liabilities, because I don’t pay the utilities, so I suspect I’ll almost always have a balance in Splitwise, hence it’s a liability.

Adding Transactions

So now that I have all of my accounts set up, it’s a matter of using the software. For this, GNUCash excels. In order to add a transaction, it’s a matter of adding a transaction to the bottom of the journal. I check my transactions with my statements each month, and make sure the numbers match. Note that because this is a double-entry bookkeeping application, each transaction you put in will appear in two accounts, deducting from one and giving to another.1

1I was confused about how money comes in and out. Here's some things that confused me:

How do I record my assets before installing GNUCash?
Everything goes into Equity::Opening Balances, which is just like a slush fund for money before the system, unless you have a record of every transaction and dollar to your name.
Where does income come from?
Income comes from the income category, and gets transferred to one of your asset accounts.
How to deal with stocks going up or dividends?
Transfer the income from the Capital Gains account into the account where the dividend is going into.
How do I deal with assets like a car or house?
Make a transaction for an asset with the amount you paid for the asset. This doesn’t make sense to me because the value of the asset will go up or down, but for personal accounting this is the standard. If doing business accounting, it can make sense to do depreciations or amortorizations, but I guess this complexity is overkill for personal finance.

The Day Book

The biggest disadvantage is the lack of a good mobile app for GNUCash. Not that it would be particularly hard to make one that is cross platform, since SQLite is literally everywhere. However, in order to deal with this downside, I have turned to a different strategy: the day book.

I now carry a notebook around everywhere, and this notebook has a pocket that I can put physical receipts into. This is really useful, because I have a singular place where I know the unreconciled receipts are.

I found that an A6 notebook works best. It so happens that I bought a Leuchttrum1917 A6 notebook back in Summer 2016, so it’s nice that I’ve finally found a better use for it—I originally used it back in high school to keep track of who has a crush on whom, but didn’t keep it up. I really like that it has a pocket not only for receipts, but also for small bits of paper that I always seem to accrue like doctor’s appointment schedules.

Also, I have a OneDrive folder with receipts for transactions that need to be added.

Budgeting

The biggest upside touted by commercial solutions is that they are really good at making budgets, and ultimately saving you money. YNAB prefers zero-based budgeting, which just means that every cent needs to be accounted for each month. Similarly, you can do the same thing in GNUCash, but the workflow is exactly the same as the rest of the app, making for a more congruent workflow. It doesn’t force you to do zero-based budgeting, but by giving you that choice, it really helps. Furthermore, it makes it easy to see how you did each month with a table view and barcharts in budgeting reports. I don’t quite know how to make the numbers line up yet, but I’ll get back to you on that.
